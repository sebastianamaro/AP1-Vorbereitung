## Core Content

### What is the EU AI Act?

The **EU AI Act** (Regulation on Artificial Intelligence) is the world's first comprehensive AI law. It regulates the use of AI systems in the European Union based on a risk-based approach.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    EU AI ACT                                    │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                 │
│   Entry into force: 2024                                       │
│   Full applicability: 2026                                     │
│                                                                 │
│   Goals:                                                       │
│   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐  │
│   │ • Protection of fundamental rights and safety            │  │
│   │ • Legal certainty for AI innovation                     │  │
│   │ • Uniform rules in the EU internal market               │  │
│   │ • Promote trust in AI                                   │  │
│   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘  │
│                                                                 │
│   Applies to:                                                  │
│   • Providers of AI systems (including from third countries)  │
│   • Operators of AI systems in the EU                         │
│   • Importers and distributors                                │
│                                                                 │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Risk-Based Approach

The EU AI Act classifies AI systems according to their risk potential into four categories.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                RISK PYRAMID                                    │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                │
│                    ▲                                           │
│                   /█\        PROHIBITED                        │
│                  /███\       Unacceptable risk                 │
│                 /█████\                                        │
│                ─────────                                       │
│               /░░░░░░░░░\    HIGH-RISK                         │
│              /░░░░░░░░░░░\   Strict requirements              │
│             /░░░░░░░░░░░░░\                                    │
│            ───────────────                                     │
│           /▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒\  LIMITED RISK                     │
│          /▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒\ Transparency obligations        │
│         /▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒▒\                                 │
│        ─────────────────────                                   │
│       /                       \ MINIMAL RISK                   │
│      /     No special          \ No requirements               │
│     /      requirements         \                              │
│    ─────────────────────────────                               │
│                                                                │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Four Risk Categories

#### 1. Unacceptable Risk (PROHIBITED)

These AI systems are prohibited in the EU.

| Prohibited AI | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Social scoring** | Evaluating citizens based on behavior |
| **Manipulation** | Subliminal manipulation of decisions |
| **Real-time biometric surveillance** | Mass facial recognition in public spaces |
| **Emotion recognition** | In workplaces and educational institutions |
| **Predictive policing** | Predicting crimes based on personality |

#### 2. High-Risk AI

These systems are allowed but subject to strict requirements.

| Area | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Critical infrastructure** | Transport, energy, water |
| **Education** | Exam evaluation, admission decisions |
| **Employment** | Application screening, performance evaluation |
| **Public authorities** | Asylum procedures, social benefits |
| **Law enforcement** | Lie detection, risk assessment |
| **Biometrics** | Facial recognition (not real-time) |

**Requirements for high-risk AI:**
- Risk management system
- Quality and data management
- Technical documentation
- Logging and monitoring
- Transparency and information
- Human oversight
- Accuracy and robustness

#### 3. Limited Risk

**Transparency obligations** apply to these systems.

| System | Transparency Obligation |
|--------|------------------------|
| **Chatbots** | Notice that AI is communicating |
| **Deepfakes** | Labeling as AI-generated |
| **Emotion recognition** | Information to users |
| **Biometric categorization** | Disclosure of use |

#### 4. Minimal Risk

No special requirements. Most AI applications fall into this category.

| Examples |
|----------|
| Spam filters |
| Recommendation systems |
| Game AI |
| Automated translation |

### Special Rules for Generative AI

Additional rules apply to **Generative AI** (like ChatGPT):

| Requirement | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Transparency** | Disclosure that content is AI-generated |
| **Copyright** | Publish summary of training data |
| **Safety** | Protective measures against illegal content |
| **Documentation** | Provide technical details |

---

### Penalties for Violations

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| **Prohibited AI practices** | Up to 35 million EUR or 7% of annual turnover |
| **High-risk requirements** | Up to 15 million EUR or 3% of annual turnover |
| **False information** | Up to 7.5 million EUR or 1.5% of annual turnover |

- **4 risk categories:** Prohibited, High-risk, Limited, Minimal

## Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1
What four risk categories does the EU AI Act distinguish?

**Solution:**
1. **Unacceptable risk (Prohibited):** Social scoring, manipulative AI
2. **High-risk:** Application screening, education, critical infrastructure
3. **Limited risk:** Chatbots, deepfakes (transparency obligations)
4. **Minimal risk:** Spam filters, recommendation systems (no requirements)

### Exercise 2
Name two examples of prohibited AI systems under the EU AI Act.

**Solution:**
1. **Social scoring:** Systems that evaluate citizens based on their behavior and restrict rights
2. **Real-time biometric surveillance:** Mass facial recognition in public spaces

(Additional: subliminal manipulation, emotion recognition in workplace)

### Exercise 3
A company uses a chatbot for customer inquiries. What obligation arises from the EU AI Act?

**Solution:** The company must fulfill a **transparency obligation**: Customers must be informed that they are communicating with an AI (and not with a human). A clear notice like "You are chatting with an AI assistant" must be displayed.

### Exercise 4
A software company develops an AI for automatic evaluation of job applications. Which risk category does this system fall into?

**Solution:** The system falls into the **high-risk** category because it is used in the employment area and makes decisions about people.

**Requirements:**
- Risk management system
- Quality assurance of training data (avoiding bias)
- Technical documentation
- Human oversight must be possible
- Logging of decisions
- CE marking before market introduction
